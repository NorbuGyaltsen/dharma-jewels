Instructions for Practicing Refuge

(These five steps are the most important elements of the practice of taking refuge. All of these elements should be included in each practice session, but you can choose to spend more time on a specific element in a particular session if you find it helpful for your practice.)

  • The first step is to reflect upon your motivation for taking refuge. Think to yourself, "I want to take refuge not only to benefit myself, but also to benefit my friends, family, and all sentient beings. I am going to practice refuge to become a buddha in order to help all sentient beings become buddhas."
  • Step two is to try to imagine the refuge tree and the sources of refuge. Begin by imagining that you are in a beautiful environment surrounded by trees, lowers, birds, green fields, etc. Within this atmosphere, imagine a pristine, clear, blue lake. From the middle of this lake grows a wish-fulfilling tree, consisting of a trunk and four branches, one in each direction. Imagine that in the middle of the wish-fulfilling tree is a lotus lower. Upon the lotus lower sits Buddha Shakyamuni, who looks upon you with great affection, smiling. His right hand covers his right knee and touches the ground. His left hand holds a bowl. Imagine the Buddha as though he is a mirage or like a reflection of the moon in a lake, as an empty appearance that is not solid or real. You can also think of Buddha Shakyamuni as representing all the buddhas, bodhisattvas, and your own teachers. The most important part of this practice is to feel the presence of the Buddha, who embodies your gurus and and the buddhas throughout the three times, so don't worry if you cannot maintain a clear visual image.
  • Step three is to reflect on the enlightened qualities of your guru, who is inseparable from the Buddha. Begin by bringing about a strong feeling of the Buddha being present in front of you. Reflect on all the enlightened qualities of the Buddha, such as wisdom, compassion, and loving-kindness. Imagine that you can actually feel these enlightened qualities radiating from the Buddha. You are taking refuge in the Buddha’s wisdom and compassion. As you reflect upon these qualities, rest in awareness from time to time.
  • Step four is to reflect upon the meaning of refuge while reciting the three-line refuge prayer and doing prostrations. Begin by getting up from your seat and place your hands in anjali at your heart-center. As you recite "Namo Buddhaya, Namo Dharmaya, Namo Sanghaya," bring your hands to the forehead, throat, and heart-center, and then perform a prostration. As you recite the refuge verses, continue this sequence.[1] While doing prostrations with your body and reciting the refuge prayer with your speech, reflect upon the meaning of taking refuge with your mind. The meaning of taking refuge is acceptance. You accept the Buddha as your guide and teacher, the Dharma as your path and practice, and the Sangha as your friends and companions on the path.
  • Step five is to contemplate the inner refuge. Begin by chanting the last line of the liturgy, beginning with, "The ultimate refuge is perfectly complete within me." Next, connect with the buddha nature within yourself by imagining that the Buddha melts into light and that this light dissolves into you. Imagine that your mind and the enlightened awareness of the Buddha merge inseparably, like water poured into water. Following this, simply rest within awareness itself. From time to time, remind yourself that your buddha nature is not different from the awakened nature of all the buddhas. Then simply rest in awareness once again.

  1. The number of prostrations or recitations you do depends on how much time you have. At a minimum, offer three in each session, though you may perform seven, twenty-one, or one hundred and eight, or more.↩︎
